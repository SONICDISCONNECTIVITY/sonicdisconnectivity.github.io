The Urban City as a Sonic Laboratory

The Project

What does global dis:connectivity sound like?

This project treats the city of Tunis as a sonic laboratory — an experimental site where the dis:connectivity of global processes becomes acoustically perceptible. Through field recordings from contrasting urban zones, it investigates how the same forces that produce connection and acoustic management in one part of the city simultaneously produce exposure, noise, and improvised substitution in another.

The city is not approached as a backdrop. It is the laboratory itself: a space where elements of global integration and disintegration can be isolated, juxtaposed, and activated as investigative tools. Listening is the method. The soundscape is the evidence.

The recordings ask: who has access to engineered silence, and who remains acoustically exposed? What global processes are registered in everyday urban sound — and what does their absence tell us about the limits of connection?

The Zones

Eight urban sites. Each a node in the acoustic map of dis:connectivity.

Recording Locations · Tunis, 2026
Zone I  ·  The Medina

Souk el Nahhassin

Liminality · Betwixt and Between

The rhythmic beating of copper and brass — craftsmen at work in passages that have held this sound for centuries. The medina is permanently betwixt and between: between the historical and the contemporary, between the formal economy and the craft tradition, between the global tourist gaze and the ancient urban fabric that long preceded it.

Zone II  ·  Rue Sidi Sofiane

Marché Sidi Bahri

Third Space · People as Infrastructure

Vendors calling out to passersby, overlapping voices, the dense energy of commercial exchange conducted entirely in the open air. Here, people are the infrastructure — their voices and collective presence constituting the market itself. The noise is not disorder. It is the sound of an economic system built from human presence rather than formal commercial architecture.

Zone III  ·  Sidi Bou Said

Touristic Café Terrace

Global Convergence · Acoustic Tourism

Café des Délices — perched above the sea, one of the most celebrated terraces in Tunisia. Folk music fills the air, voices from many countries overlap, and Tunisian musical heritage becomes an acoustic spectacle offered to the world. This is sound curated for global encounter: managed, staged, performed. A very different kind of acoustic management from the residential silence nearby — not the absence of sound, but its deliberate production.

Zone IV  ·  Sidi Bou Said

Quiet Neighborhood

Engineered Silence · Acoustic Privilege

A residential street away from the tourist circuit. The soundscape thins to almost nothing: the breath of the air, the occasional voice of a young person passing, jasmine scattered across the ground by the wind. This silence is not natural — it is produced through urban planning, property values, and the careful ordering of space. Recorded at the same time of day as Marché Sidi Bahri, it is the acoustic form of the same global forces that generate the noise elsewhere.

Zone V  ·  Sidi Bou Said

From Quiet to Touristic Zone

Liminality · The Threshold in Motion

A walk recorded in real time: leaving the quiet residential neighborhood and moving toward the touristic zone. The acoustic transition is gradual then sudden — engineered silence gives way to the managed sound of global encounter. The same small geography contains two radically different acoustic worlds. This recording is the warp made audible: dis:connectivity held in a single, unbroken walk.

Theoretical Framework

The Concepts

Roland Wenzlhuemer

Dis:connectivity

The world does not shrink uniformly through globalisation — it warps. The same infrastructure that connects one zone simultaneously disconnects another. Dis:connectivity names this turbulent, mutually constitutive relationship: the engineered silence of Sidi Bou Said and the acoustic density of working-class neighborhoods are not two separate phenomena. They are produced together, by the same forces, at the same moment.

Homi K. Bhabha

The Third Space

In postcolonial urban contexts, most places exist neither fully inside global circuits nor fully outside them. Bhabha's Third Space names this permanent structural condition — hybrid, unstable, generative. The working-class neighborhoods of Tunis are not transitioning toward full integration. They permanently inhabit the in-between: produced by global forces but not fully served by them.

AbdouMaliq Simone

People as Infrastructure

Where formal infrastructure is inadequate, people build their own. Vendors, craftsmen, and market workers create collective improvised systems that substitute for what formal development does not provide. In Tunis, the acoustic density of working-class streets is not disorder — it is people functioning as infrastructure. The noise is the sound of collective agency in the Third Space.

Steven Feld

Acoustemology

Acoustemology — acoustic epistemology — is the claim that listening is a form of knowing, not merely a form of hearing. Sound does not illustrate knowledge produced elsewhere; it produces its own knowledge. When we listen to Tunisian soundscapes as an experimental method, we are not decorating an argument already made in text. We are conducting an inquiry that text alone cannot perform.

The Researcher

About

Mohamed Ali Kechiche
Tunis, Tunisia

Mohamed Ali Kechiche (TUN — he/him) is a classical pianist, musicologist, and anthropologist from Tunis, Tunisia. He holds an undergraduate degree in Music & Interpretation (Piano Performance) from the Higher Institute of Music of Tunis. He is currently pursuing a Master's Degree in Music & Musicology at the Higher Institute of Music of Tunis and a Master's Degree in Social & Cultural Anthropology at the Higher Institute of Human Sciences of Tunis. Coming from a family of musicians, he approaches music in an intellectually engaged manner, seeking not only to interpret repertoire but to contextualize it through historical, cultural, and analytical lenses.

His training includes piano studies with prestigious professors such as the late Dr. Ralph Wilson Cooper, Füsun Güray Regaieg, Dr. Mehdi Trabelsi, and Dr. Natalia Tchernykh. His repertoire spans a wide range of styles and eras, with a particular focus on the works of J.S. Bach, Beethoven, Liszt, and Bartók.

Alongside piano performance and musicology, Ali maintains a multidisciplinary interest in philosophy, cultural studies, history, and the arts. He is particularly interested in ethnomusicology and postcolonial studies, which inform his understanding of music's social functions and global contexts. As an educator, he has taught piano and music in various settings, with an emphasis on both technical development and interpretive depth.

In 2023, he was selected as a fellow for the "Tawasol: Cultural Production and Policy Network", as part of which he co-developed a documentary exploring Cairo's underground art scene and the resilience of independent artists navigating complex cultural and political landscapes.
